1. THE ANATOMY OF A BOWL — the modular model  [VERIFIED 3-0]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

A bowl is a deliberately MODULAR construction along independent axes. Canonical
FIVE components:
  1. Noodles (麺 men)
  2. Broth / soup (だし/スープ) — the simmered stock
  3. Tare (タレ) — concentrated seasoning base
  4. Aroma oil (香味油 kōmi-abura / kaori-abura) — fragrance/mouthfeel oil
  5. Toppings (具 gu)

The broth and tare are kept SEPARATE on purpose: one broth pot can yield shoyu / shio /
miso variants just by swapping the tare. This separation is the single most important
concept for classifying a shop — broth base and tare are TWO different axes.

BROTH (スープ) — built essentially unsalted; flavor identity comes from the tare.
  Caveat (verified nuance): "completely unseasoned" is an idealized principle. Broths
  routinely carry aromatics (onion, ginger, kombu, shiitake, scallion) and a minority of
  shops salt the broth directly. So treat "unseasoned broth" as the design intent, not an
  absolute. [The over-literal "flavor-neutral stock" phrasing was REFUTED 0-3.]

TARE (タレ) — the concentrated seasoning that defines the bowl's final flavor and gives
  it its NAME. Three main types:
    • shoyu (醤油) — soy-sauce based; typically a liquid concentrate
    • shio (塩) — salt based; typically a liquid concentrate
    • miso (味噌) — fermented soybean paste; a more complex paste-based prep
  ⚠ IMPORTANT CLASSIFICATION RULE: **tonkotsu is a BROTH, not a tare.** Do not list
  tonkotsu as a tare type. A bowl can be e.g. tonkotsu-broth + shoyu-tare.

AROMA OIL (香味油) — a distinct finishing component added with the tare. It does NOT
  emulsify into the water-based broth; it floats, forming light-reflecting droplets on
  the surface. Common types:
    • chiyu (鶏油) — rendered chicken fat
    • lard / se-abura (背脂) — pork back fat (often as floating pellets)
    • mayu (マー油) — black/burnt garlic oil
    • negi oil (ネギ油) — scallion oil
  [All VERIFIED 3-0.]

2. NOODLES & KANSUI  [VERIFIED 3-0]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Ramen noodles = wheat flour + water + salt + **kansui** (かん水), the alkaline salt
solution that distinguishes ramen noodles from all other wheat noodles.
  • Composition: mainly potassium carbonate (K2CO3) + sodium carbonate (Na2CO3),
    sometimes with phosphates. Common ratio ~7:3 K:Na (a.k.a. "AKA/red kansui" cited as
    60% potassium / 40% sodium — same ratio, two phrasings). Used at ~1–1.5% of flour weight.
  • Effect: raises dough pH to ~9, which (a) turns wheat's flavone pigments YELLOW (the
    golden hue), (b) strengthens the gluten network (disulfide bonding) → the firm, springy
    "koshi" (コシ) chew, (c) adds a subtle alkaline note. The two salts pull opposite ways:
    potassium carbonate HARDENS (and yellows more); sodium carbonate SOFTENS.

NOODLE ↔ BROTH MATCHING (a real classification signal):
  • Thin noodles use more potassium carbonate (hardness); thick noodles use more sodium
    carbonate (avoid excess hardness).
  • Hydration (加水率 kasui-ritsu) varies by style. Hakata is extremely LOW-hydration
    (~22–28%) — so stiff it's effectively impossible to hand-knead (traditionally
    foot-stepped / machine-rolled). Low-hydration thin straight noodles suit heavy
    tonkotsu; higher-hydration wavy noodles often pair with lighter/clear soups.
  ⚠ Do NOT assert a simple "higher water = softer, lower water = floury" monotonic rule —
    that simplistic mapping was REFUTED (1-2). Hydration interacts with kansui, thickness,
    and rest/aging; describe per-shop specifics, not a formula.

Terms worth capturing per shop: 多加水 (ta-kasui, high-hydration) vs 低加水 (tei-kasui,
low-hydration); 自家製麺 (jikasei-men, house-made noodles) vs outsourced; thickness;
straight vs 縮れ麺 (chijire-men, wavy/crimped).

3. REGIONAL DEEP-DIVES VERIFIED THIS ROUND
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

HAKATA TONKOTSU (博多豚骨)  [VERIFIED 3-0]
  Pork bones boiled hard ~18–20h into a creamy, white, emulsified broth; very thin,
  straight, low-hydration noodles. Two signature ordering rituals:
    • kaedama (替え玉) — extra noodle refills, ~¥150–200.
    • noodle-firmness ladder (softest→hardest): yawa (やわ) → futsu (futsū 普通, normal)
      → kata (かた, firm) → barikata (バリかた, very firm) → harigane (針金, "wire") →
      konaotoshi (粉落とし, flash-dipped, "flour-dropping").
  (Boil time varies shop to shop; 8–20h+ all attested.)

KYOTO RAMEN (京都ラーメン) — THREE lineages, NOT one  [VERIFIED 3-0]
  Kyoto is emphatically NOT a single style. Document it as three lineages:
    1. Light/simple chicken-pork shoyu — clear-ish, restrained (e.g. Shinpuku Saikan
       新福菜館 — known dark soy; Honke Daiichi Asahi 本家第一旭).
    2. Se-abura (背脂) shoyu — the "standard" Kyoto style: shoyu soup under a layer of
       pork back fat (e.g. Masutani ますたに, pioneered ~1947; Hosokawa).
    3. Kotteri (こってり) gelatinous — ultra-thick from extended chicken boiling
       (Tenkaippin 天下一品, 1970s).
  ⚠ Two "Kyoto = one seabura-shoyu style" framings were REFUTED (0-3, 1-2). Always
    treat Kyoto as three lineages.

4. HISTORY (orientation)  [VERIFIED 3-0]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Chinese wheat-noodle roots reach Japan in the 17th c. (attributed to Tokugawa Mitsukuni
receiving them from Ming scholar Zhu Shunsui/Zhu Zhiyu — though those used lotus-root
starch + wheat, not modern kansui chūkamen). The dish won over Japanese diners in the
Meiji era (1868–1912); first specialized shop Rairaiken (来々軒) opened 1910 in Asakusa.
It became an everyday national dish POST-WWII, spread by returnees from China amid
wheat-flour abundance.

5. BROTH TEXTURE AXIS — chintan vs paitan  [VERIFIED 3-0]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Independent of broth-base ingredient, ramen broth splits into two TEXTURAL classes — a
key classification axis:
  • 清湯 (chintan) — CLEAR broth, gently simmered so it stays translucent (e.g. Tokyo
    shoyu, Hakodate shio, Onomichi).
  • 白湯 (paitan) — CLOUDY/emulsified broth, hard-boiled so fat & collagen emulsify into a
    creamy opaque soup (e.g. Hakata tonkotsu, tori-paitan chicken).
Pair this with the richness axis assari (あっさり, light) ↔ kotteri (こってり, rich/heavy).
A bowl is well-described by: broth BASE (pork/chicken/seafood/…) × TEXTURE (chintan/paitan)
× TARE (shoyu/shio/miso) × richness. (Tori-paitan = cloudy chicken; the clear-chicken
counterpart is chicken chintan.)

6. REGIONAL PANTHEON — verified styles (NATIONAL; a shop anywhere may serve any of these)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

There are ~185 recognized gotōchi (ご当地) regional styles on the Japan Ramen Association's
2025 national list — the pantheon is large; below are the ones verified this round. (Per
Greg: classify a shop's `styles` from the WHOLE-Japan catalogue, not the city it sits in.)

HOKKAIDO — the "three great" (北海道三大ラーメン): Sapporo, Hakodate, Asahikawa.  [VERIFIED 3-0]
  • SAPPORO MISO (札幌味噌) — pork-bone broth + MISO tare + a lid of floating lard (built to
    hold heat in the cold); high-hydration medium-thick WAVY noodles (多加水中太縮れ麺).
    Miso ramen was devised in 1955. Tell: opaque miso soup, often with stir-fried
    beansprouts/corn/butter, lard sheen.
  • HAKODATE SHIO (函館塩) — CLEAR (chintan) low-fat pork-bone broth + SALT tare;
    high-hydration medium-thin STRAIGHT noodles (the straight noodle is distinctive in
    wavy-dominant Hokkaido). Tell: pale clear salt soup.
  • ASAHIKAWA SHOYU (旭川醤油) — DOUBLE soup (fish + pork-bone & chicken/veg) + SOY tare +
    a layer of BURNT LARD forming an insulating oil film; low-hydration medium-thin WAVY
    noodles (低加水中細縮れ麺). Tell: dark soy soup with an oil cap.

TOHOKU:
  • KITAKATA (喜多方, Fukushima) — transparent SHOYU broth; flat, AGED, high-hydration
    noodles (平打ち熟成多加水麺). One of Japan's "three major" ramen towns. Tell: wide
    flat wavy noodles in clear soy soup; famous for asa-ra (morning ramen).

KYUSHU — the tonkotsu family (tell them apart by origin + richness):  [VERIFIED 3-0]
  • KURUME (久留米) — the BIRTHPLACE of tonkotsu (stall "Nankin Senryo", 1937). Earliest
    broth was light-colored; the now-iconic CLOUDY WHITE style was an accident in 1947
    (Katsumi Sugino over-boiled the pot). Richest/strongest pork fragrance.
  • HAKATA (博多, Fukuoka) — the nationally-famous version; creamy white emulsified tonkotsu
    + very thin straight LOW-hydration noodles; kaedama + the yawa→konaotoshi firmness
    ladder (see §3). Synthesized Kurume's cloudy broth + Nagahama's thin-noodle/kaedama system.
  • NAGAHAMA (長浜) — Hakata's fishing-port sibling; ULTRA-thin noodles for fast service;
    ORIGINATED the kaedama (替え玉) refill system.

CHUGOKU / app-region styles:
  • ONOMICHI (尾道, near Hiroshima) — CLEAR chicken-bone (tori-gara) + Seto Inland Sea
    small-fish dashi, SHOYU tare; the TELL = chunks of minced pork BACK-FAT (背脂) floating
    on a clear soup (kotteri despite being clear); medium-thin/fine, LOW-hydration,
    FLAT-CUT (平打ち) noodles. (Shop variation: some use pork-bone+seafood w/ thin straight
    noodles, others add chicken+veg+kombu.)

7. APP-CITY LOCAL STYLES & SUB-GENRES  [VERIFIED 3-0]
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

HIROSHIMA (app city) has TWO signature noodle dishes — both are app-relevant and neither
is a normal soup ramen:
  • 広島つけ麺 (Hiroshima tsukemen) — distinguished from Kanto tsukemen by BOTH the noodles
    AND the dipping sauce being served COLD/CHILLED. Soy-based dip with chili oil (rayu),
    red pepper, vinegar & sesame; heat in a SEVEN-STEP scale from mild to 激辛 (extra rayu
    addable). Served with boiled cabbage + a boiled egg. Originally called "reimen" (冷麺)
    before the "Hiroshima tsukemen" name spread; traces to a post-WWII Hatchobori Chinese
    restaurant that served seasonal (Mar–Oct) reimen.
  • 汁なし担々麺 (shiru-nashi tantanmen) — BROTH-LESS dandan noodles; the character is
    花椒 (Sichuan-pepper numbing) + ラー油 (chili-oil heat), pork mince, mix vigorously
    (~30×) before eating. KISAKU (きさく) is credited as the pioneer that sparked the boom;
    KUNIMATSU (くにまつ, Hatchobori, 2009) popularized & spread it. (King-ken is another
    well-known chain.)

NARA: TENRI STAMINA RAMEN (天理スタミナラーメン, "Tensuta" 天スタ) — a genuine Nara-area
  local style (Tenri City, since 1978; company Nakai). A tonkotsu/chicken-bone SHOYU broth
  topped with a spicy STIR-FRY of pork, Chinese cabbage & nira (chives) with garlic +
  doubanjiang/chili, plus back fat. Spicy, garlicky, hearty. (It's just outside central
  Nara but is the prefecture's defining bowl.)

KANAZAWA: no strong indigenous ramen style surfaced; the notable local marker is HACHIBAN
  RAMEN (8番らーめん) — a HOKURIKU regional chain concentrated in Ishikawa/Toyama/Fukui/
  Nagano (+Okayama), not a nationwide one (vegetable-topped). Treat Kanazawa ramen shops
  mostly as nationwide/standard styles rather than a local school.

8. GLUTEN-FREE / VEGAN REALITY (ramen)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

GLUTEN-FREE: essentially ALL ramen is unsafe for celiacs — the noodles are wheat+kansui,
shoyu tare is wheat-based soy sauce, miso may contain barley/wheat, and gyoza/most toppings
add wheat. Treat ramen as NOT celiac-safe by default; a genuine GF bowl needs rice/konjac
noodles + GF tamari tare + controlled broth, which is rare and must be shop-confirmed.

VEGAN: the hidden trap is dashi — katsuo/niboshi (fish) stock pervades even "vegetable"
soups; chashu, ajitama, lard, and chiyu are animal. A truly vegan bowl needs a
plant broth (kombu + shiitake + vegetables) with NO fish dashi and NO animal tare/oil/
toppings. VERIFIED example: TOWZEN (Kyoto) serves a fully vegan SOY-MILK ramen — soy-milk
broth flavored with kombu + soy sauce, NO fish dashi. These exist but are specialist; verify.

9. NARRATIVE PHILOSOPHY — "ramen theology" (Greg, 2026-06-23)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
A lens for how the ramen feature should SPEAK (drives the `pantheon` narrative + bios):
  • BROTH = the SOUL — it carries the regional lineage. You sip a Hakata bowl and taste its
    ancestry (chanpon, 2 AM yatai yaki-ramen); the soup is *what makes it Hakata*.
  • NOODLES = the BODY.
  • CHASHU = the HEART (its own schema field, not a topping).
  • TOPPINGS = the CLOTHES / accessories — how the bowl presents itself to the world.

THE KEY PRINCIPLE (regional vs shop): a regional style's identity lives in the SHARED
soul + body + heart. Individual shops differ in the ACCESSORIES (more/less negi, moyashi-
vs cabbage-forward) — dressed differently, but still unmistakably, say, a Hakata bowl.
→ The pass should write the soul/body/heart as the regional truth (what makes it its style)
  and the toppings/notes as the SHOP's individual signature. The radar profile is mostly a
  "soul fingerprint" (broth axes) + body (noodle_firmness).

EXEMPLAR of a soul-first writeup — ラーメン親爺 (Ramen Oyaji, Kyoto/Hanazono): the bowl
"rests entirely on its broth." It LOOKS heavy and dark (black/brown), but tastes
surprisingly LIGHT and PROFOUNDLY DEEP at once — because owner Katagiri builds the broth
from pork MEAT rather than bones. That contrast (dark look ≠ heavy taste; meat-not-bone
soul) is exactly the kind of insight the `pantheon`/bio text should surface. (Greg's
first-hand knowledge — a DCP candidate; see research/human_ramen_notes.md.)
